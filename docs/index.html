Growth Funnel & Retention Analysis (E-commerce Case Study)

Oct-Nov 2019 | DuckDB + Parquet | Funnel | Retention Cohorts | Revenue & Observed LTV

Business Summary: This case study explains what drove revenue growth between October and November 2019 and where conversion quality weakened. The focus is funnel efficiency, cohort retention, and monetization mix to support product, lifecycle, and merchandising decisions.

  • View-to-cart rose from 11.2% to 22.4%, while cart-to-purchase dropped from 60.2% to 48.4%
  • Revenue grew +19.7% MoM, with 95.2% of November revenue concentrated in high-price items
  • Retention decays early: average repeat falls from 13.3% (week 1) to 10.2% (week 2)
  • New cohorts are less valuable early: observed LTV declines from $1,449 to $394 in the latest cohort

Executive Highlights

Conversion

View-to-cart increased from 11.2% to 22.4% (+11.2 pp), but cart-to-purchase fell from 60.2% to 48.4% (-11.7 pp).

Decision lens: checkout reliability is now the main conversion bottleneck.

Retention

Average repeat declines from 13.3% in week 1 to 10.2% in week 2 (-3.1 pp), with weaker week-1 retention in newer cohorts.

Decision lens: improve post-purchase lifecycle in the first 14 days.

Monetization

November revenue grew +19.7% MoM, with 95.2% concentrated in high-price products; newer cohorts show lower observed early LTV.

Decision lens: protect premium conversion and raise value in recent cohorts.

Key Visuals

Business evidence by priority area: conversion, retention, and monetization quality.

01) Funnel Conversion and Volume Dynamics

Left: conversion by stage. Right: user volume by stage.

What happened: View-to-cart rose from 11.2% to 22.4%, while cart-to-purchase declined from 60.2% to 48.4%.

Why it likely happened: Cart users grew +145% versus +22% growth in viewers, suggesting a much wider top-of-funnel intent capture than checkout capacity. In parallel, purchase-without-cart share dropped from 41.6% to 9.4%, indicating stronger cart-step capture in November and a larger cart denominator.

Business implication: Priority should shift to checkout reliability and payment-step diagnostics to recover purchase efficiency.

02) Cohort Retention Quality

Left: retention heatmap by cohort week. Right: decay curve from week 1 onward (focus on repeat behavior after the first purchase).

What happened: Repeat purchase drops from 13.3% in week 1 to 10.2% in week 2, and November cohorts start below October cohorts at week 1 (-3.6 pp).

Why it likely happened: Cohort quality appears weaker in November (week 1: 11.1% vs 14.6% in October cohorts; week 2: 6.4% vs 11.7%), consistent with broader acquisition and lower early intent-to-repeat behavior.

Business implication: The first 14 days post-purchase are the highest leverage window for CRM, replenishment triggers, and second-order incentives.

03) Revenue Mix and Cohort Monetization

Left: revenue mix by price segment. Right: observed LTV trend by cohort week with cohort-size context.

What happened: Revenue grew +19.7% MoM, with 95.2% of November revenue concentrated in high-price products; low-price contribution remains very small.

Why it likely happened: High-price segment contributed most of growth (+$42.3M of +$45.2M total growth), led by categories such as smartphones. At cohort level, observed LTV trends downward in newer cohorts even when cohort sizes vary, suggesting monetization quality pressure over time.

Business implication: Protect premium conversion while building repeat value in newer cohorts to reduce dependence on a narrow monetization base.

Note: "Why it likely happened" statements are evidence-based hypotheses from behavioral patterns in the dataset, not causal proofs.
